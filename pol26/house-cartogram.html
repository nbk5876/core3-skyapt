All 435 House seats are drawn below — one square each. Most are not in doubt. The chamber is decided by the few dozen outlined in bold: tap any of them for who holds it now and how close it was in 2024.

Republicans hold a 219–212 majority with four vacant seats. Democrats need a net gain of three to flip the House — forecasters rate the chamber a genuine toss-up.

Why squares, not a real map? A geographic map makes empty rural districts look huge and packs dozens of city seats into a dot — it distorts the very thing we’re counting. One square per seat shows the chamber as it actually divides. And with several states redrawing their maps mid-decade (Texas, California, North Carolina, Ohio, Missouri, Utah), the safe seats are shown in aggregate; the competitive ones, where the verified detail lives, are individual and tappable.
